# Bitcoin Halving History: What Happened After Each Supply Cut

*By Jay, Bitcoin investor since 2017* · March 2026

The Bitcoin halving is the single most important structural event in cryptocurrency markets. Every ~210,000 blocks — approximately every four years — the reward that miners receive for adding a new block to the blockchain is cut in half. This reduction in new supply has preceded every major Bitcoin bull market in history. Here is the complete timeline.

## The Halving Mechanism

Bitcoin's total supply is capped at 21 million coins. New coins enter circulation through mining — the process of validating transactions and adding blocks to the blockchain. The halving cuts the rate of new supply by 50%, creating a supply shock: fewer new coins entering the market while demand remains the same or increases.

Critically, miners must sell a portion of their Bitcoin to cover operational costs (electricity, hardware, facilities). When their revenue is halved overnight but costs stay constant, the net selling pressure from miners drops dramatically. This structural reduction in sell-side pressure, combined with steady or growing demand, creates the conditions for price appreciation.

## First Halving — November 28, 2012

Bitcoin was barely known outside cryptography circles. The price at the time of the first halving was approximately $12. Over the following year, Bitcoin surged to $1,163 by late November 2013 — a roughly 9,600% gain.

This cycle introduced the world to Bitcoin's boom-and-bust pattern. After the peak, Bitcoin corrected sharply, losing about 85% of its value over the next year and not recovering to the $1,000 level until early 2017.

The 2012 halving established the template: supply cut → lag period → parabolic rise → sharp correction → extended bear market → repeat.

## Second Halving — July 9, 2016

By 2016, Bitcoin had a growing community and nascent exchange infrastructure. The price at the second halving was approximately $650. Over the next 17 months, Bitcoin climbed to $19,783 by December 17, 2017 — roughly a 2,940% gain.

The 2017 bull run was the cycle that brought Bitcoin into mainstream consciousness. Media coverage exploded, retail investors flooded in, and "Bitcoin" became a household word. ICOs (Initial Coin Offerings) added speculative fuel.

The subsequent crash was brutal: Bitcoin fell from $19,783 to $3,200 by December 2018 — an 84% decline. The bear market lasted approximately 12 months from the peak.

## Third Halving — May 11, 2020

The third halving occurred during the COVID-19 pandemic. Bitcoin had crashed to $3,800 in March 2020 during the liquidity crisis, then recovered to approximately $8,600 by the halving in May.

This cycle was different in character. Institutional adoption accelerated: MicroStrategy, Tesla, and Square added Bitcoin to their balance sheets. PayPal enabled Bitcoin purchases for 350 million users. The narrative shifted from "speculative asset" to "digital gold" and "inflation hedge."

Bitcoin reached $69,000 in November 2021 — approximately a 700% gain from the halving price. The subsequent bear market drove prices to $16,000 by late 2022, a 77% decline from the peak.

## Fourth Halving — April 19, 2024

The most recent halving is unique in Bitcoin's history: for the first time, Bitcoin had already exceeded its previous all-time high before the halving event. BTC was trading near $64,000 when the block reward dropped from 6.25 to 3.125 BTC.

Several factors distinguish this cycle:

- **Spot Bitcoin ETFs** were approved in the US in January 2024, providing institutional access at unprecedented scale.
- **Mining profitability pressure** is acute — with a reward of only 3.125 BTC per block, miners must be extraordinarily efficient to remain profitable, leading to industry consolidation.
- **Market maturity** — derivatives, options, and sophisticated trading infrastructure mean the market absorbs information faster than in previous cycles.

As of March 2026, approximately 687 days have elapsed since the fourth halving. This cycle is ongoing.

## The Diminishing Returns Pattern

Each successive cycle has produced smaller percentage gains from halving to peak:

- 1st cycle: ~9,600%
- 2nd cycle: ~2,940%
- 3rd cycle: ~700%
- 4th cycle: Ongoing

This is expected. As Bitcoin's market capitalization grows, it requires proportionally more capital to produce the same percentage move. Moving a $10 billion asset by 10x requires $100 billion of net buying. Moving a $1 trillion asset by 10x requires $10 trillion — orders of magnitude more capital.

Diminishing percentage returns does not mean diminishing value. A 200% gain on a $60,000 base price produces $120,000 in absolute gains per coin — a larger dollar move than the 2017 cycle's entire run from $650 to $20,000.

## What About 2028?

The fifth halving is expected around March 2028, when the block reward will drop to 1.5625 BTC. Based on the historical pattern:

- **Supply impact** will be smaller in absolute terms — cutting daily issuance from ~450 BTC to ~225 BTC.
- **Diminishing returns** suggest a smaller percentage gain than the 4th cycle, but potentially still significant in dollar terms.
- **Structural maturity** — by 2028, Bitcoin's market infrastructure will be even more developed, potentially dampening volatility while supporting larger capital flows.

The 2028 halving will be the first where the block reward falls below 2 BTC. At some point, halvings may cease to produce the dramatic supply shocks of earlier cycles as block rewards become a smaller fraction of total trading volume. Whether that point is 2028 or later remains to be seen.

## Key Takeaways

- Every halving has been followed by a significant bull market, but with diminishing percentage returns.
- The time from halving to peak has ranged from 12 to 18 months across the three completed cycles.
- Each cycle's bear market has lasted approximately 12 months from peak to trough, with 77–85% drawdowns.
- The 2024 cycle is unique: first halving with a pre-existing ATH and institutional ETF infrastructure.
- Past patterns are a framework, not a guarantee. Each cycle brings new variables.
